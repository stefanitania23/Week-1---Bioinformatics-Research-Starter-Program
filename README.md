# Week 1 - Paper Summary on Transcriptomic Analysis of the Effect of Glabridin on Biofilm Formation in *Staphylococcus aureus*
*Staphylococcus aureus* is a major pathogen responsible for infections in humans and animals, primarily due to its ability to form biofilms that enhance antibiotic resistance and trigger chronic infections, including cases involving methicillin-resistant *Staphylococcus aureus* (MRSA). The irrational use of antibiotics at subinhibitory concentrations, along with the prolonged development of new antibiotics, highlights the need for safer and more effective alternative antibiofilm agents. Glabridin (Glb), a flavonoid extracted from *Glycyrrhiza glabra* (licorice), has been reported to exhibit antibacterial activity and potential effectiveness against resistant strains. However, the molecular mechanism underlying glabridin’s inhibition of biofilm formation has not been fully elucidated. Therefore, this study aims to investigate the effect of glabridin on *S. aureus* biofilm formation through a transcriptomic approach and laboratory assays.

In this study, *Staphylococcus aureus* was treated with glabridin at subinhibitory concentrations of ½ MIC, ¼ MIC, and ⅛ MIC, with the MIC value of glabridin determined to be 8 µg/mL. Biofilm formation was analyzed using the crystal violet assay to quantify biofilm biomass, scanning electron microscopy (SEM) to observe morphological changes, and an extracellular DNA (eDNA) inhibition assay to assess a major component of the biofilm matrix. The results demonstrated that glabridin significantly reduced *S. aureus* biofilm formation in a dose-dependent manner. SEM observations revealed that glabridin disrupted early bacterial adhesion and cell accumulation, as well as damaged the biofilm structure, indicated by reduced cell clustering and disorganized biofilm architecture. Furthermore, glabridin at subinhibitory concentrations significantly suppressed eDNA secretion, contributing to decreased biofilm stability and integrity.
